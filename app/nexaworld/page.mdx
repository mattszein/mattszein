# NexaWorld Manifesto 🌍

<br />

## How I Got Here

In 2012 I watched a documentary about what really happens to animals in the food industry. The reality was undeniable: animals suffer immensely because of human choices. That realization didn't just change what I ate, it pushed me to question assumptions about everything.

If I had been so wrong about something so fundamental, what else was I wrong about? I started questioning our actions, our values, what pushes us to act the way we do, what truly drives us.

The questions multiplied: How do we organize society? Which technologies serve us versus exploit us?

At some point in this search, I discovered Jacque Fresco's Venus Project: a vision of resource-based economies, sustainable technology, robotic automation, and cities designed for human flourishing rather than capital extraction. Here was someone who'd thought systematically about redesigning social infrastructure from the ground up.

The vision resonated. But the transition question haunted me: How do we get there from here?
<br />

## The Paradox

Humanity holds the tools to eradicate poverty, hunger, and unnecessary suffering... yet billions still lack their most basic needs. Our societies remain organized around outdated systems of privilege, profit, and corruption, while our greatest asset — human creativity — is underused, wasted on repetitive or meaningless work.

We waste human potential at scale. Most jobs today drain creativity rather than cultivate it. Meanwhile, we have the technology and resources to meet everyone's basic needs, but our current social infrastructure wasn't specifically designed for that purpose.
<br />

## The Open Source Lesson: What Really Drives Innovation?

Here's the standard objection: "But capitalism drives innovation! Without the profit motive, without competition, why would anyone do anything?"
What has driven some of the most transformative software progress in the last 30 years?
<br />

-> Linux, the operating system that powers the majority of the world's servers, smartphones (Android), and supercomputers. Created by a Finnish student who just wanted to make something better. Maintained by thousands of contributors who receive no direct payment.
Linux isn't an isolated case. Consider how software actually gets built today. When Linus Torvalds got frustrated with existing version control systems in 2005, he didn't wait for a company to solve it: he spent a weekend building Git. No business plan. Just a programmer annoyed by a problem, who happened to have the skills to fix it. Today, Git is how all modern software development happens.
<br />
-> Guido van Rossum was working at a research institute in Amsterdam in 1989, and over Christmas break - his vacation time - he started writing a new programming language. Why? Because he thought programming could be more intuitive, more readable, more human-friendly. No profit motive. Just the conviction that things could be better. Today, Python is one of the world's most popular languages, powering everything from Instagram to scientific research to AI development.
<br />
-> David Heinemeier Hansson was building a project management tool called Basecamp when he realized he'd created something more interesting than the product itself, a framework that made web development dramatically simpler. He could have kept it proprietary, could have built a business around it. Instead, in 2004, he open-sourced Ruby on Rails. The result? It democratized web development. Thousands of startups that couldn't afford expensive development infrastructure could suddenly build sophisticated applications.
<br />
This pattern isn't new.
<br />
-> Darwin spent years after the Beagle voyage thinking about the patterns he'd observed. He wasn't funded to develop a theory of evolution. He was driven by intellectual obsession. When he finally published On the Origin of Species, it wasn't because he'd hit a profit target. It was because the evidence demanded a new explanation.
<br />
-> Or Einstein in 1905, the "miracle year." He was working at the Swiss patent office, not at a university. Physics was what he did in his spare time because he couldn't stop thinking about light, about time, about how the universe worked. Four papers that year changed physics forever.
This pattern repeats everywhere you look.
<br />
Transformative innovations often come from people who are intrinsically motivated: by curiosity, by the joy of solving problems, by artistic vision, by the desire to make something useful and share it.

When people have their basic needs met and time to themselves, what do they actually do? They learn instruments... not for money, but because music brings joy. They write stories no one may read. They build elaborate structures in Minecraft or other games. They contribute to open source projects. They volunteer in their communities. They master difficult physical skills through sport. They explore philosophical questions that have no "practical" application. They teach others what they know, because sharing knowledge feels good.
<br />
The talent is here. The motivation is here. But we are still living in a system that channels it toward profit extraction rather than human flourishing.
<br />
"But competition drives excellence!" Yes, sometimes. Athletes competing push each other to new heights. Companies competing can spur innovation.
<br />
But the open source model revealed something: it enables collaboration and human ingenuity compounds. My improvement builds on your improvement builds on someone else's insight. We all get better faster together than we ever could alone. Science works this way. Research builds on research. Yes, people want recognition. They want their contributions valued. They want to master difficult skills and earn respect from peers. But none of that requires artificial scarcity or threats of poverty.
<br />

## What Changed?

A century ago, meeting everyone's basic needs wasn't impossible, it was impractical given the constraints we faced. Agricultural productivity demanded massive human labor. Manufacturing required hands-on work at every stage. Energy sources were limited and expensive to distribute. Nearly everyone had to spend most of their time on survival labor just to maintain the system.

More than that, our social structures, values, and institutions evolved around this reality. When most people must work to eat, economies organized around labor made sense. When production was constrained by human capacity, competition for resources seemed necessary. The system we inherited wasn't designed by malice, it emerged from genuine material constraints.

What's different now? We have automation that can handle repetitive labor that once required human effort. We have the technology.

The scarcity is increasingly artificial, maintained because our economic system requires it to function. We have empty houses and homeless people in the same cities. We destroy excess food while people go hungry. Not because we lack resources, but because distribution is optimized for profit extraction, not human welfare.

We crossed a threshold where the old assumptions no longer apply the same way.
<br />

## The Vision: Self-Sustaining Mini-Eco-Cities 🌱

NexaWorld is born from the conviction that we can design a better society, piece by piece, city by city.

This is a vision, a blueprint, and a commitment: to design and build self-sustaining, technologically advanced mini-cities where human flourishing is the central goal. We do not propose utopia. We propose a science-based approach, rooted in skepticism, evidence, and effective altruism: decisions guided by data, experimentation, and measurable impact.

The premise is simple: if technology can meet our basic needs sustainably, then society can focus on human potential.

Nexaworld is a framework for experimentation. Each implementation will differ based on location, resources, scale, and the people involved. What unites them: commitment to human wellbeing as the design constraint, evidence-based decision making, and open sharing of learnings.
<br />

## The Foundations 🏛️

<br />

### Evidence Over Doctrine

We don't claim to have all the answers, and that's the point. Every decision must be tested against data and evidence. Policies, design, and growth must be guided by research, trials, and measurable impact. This means we might be wrong. We might discover that something we believed would work... doesn't. And that is okay. But we'll know it, measure it, and correct it.

What does this look like in practice? Imagine we're deciding between two renewable energy systems. One feels more natural, more aligned with romantic ideas about sustainability. The other is less aesthetically pleasing but produces 40% more reliable energy with less maintenance. Which do we choose? The data decides, not the ideology.

Take food production. Vertical farming sounds ideal: controlled environment, minimal land use, year-round production. But what if the energy costs don't pencil out in certain climates? What if hydroponics works better for leafy greens but aeroponics is more efficient for root vegetables? We don't decide based on which sounds cooler. We test, measure energy input vs nutritional output vs taste vs maintenance costs, and let the evidence guide us.

Or governance. Should decisions be made by direct democracy? Representative systems? Consensus? Liquid democracy where you can delegate your vote on topics you don't understand? We don't know! So we try different models in different contexts, measure satisfaction, efficiency, fairness, and adapt. Does this actually work for the people living here?
<br />

### Transparency, Accountability, and Cooperation

What happens when someone makes a mistake? What happens when a system isn't working? What happens when someone has a better idea?

In most organizations, the answer involves politics, hierarchy, blame, and resistance to change. Information gets controlled. Decisions get made behind closed doors. Questioning authority is discouraged.

But what if transparency was the default? What if all data about resource use, energy consumption, decision-making processes, and outcomes was openly available? Not to create surveillance, but to enable collective intelligence. When everyone can see how the system works, everyone can help improve it.

This means accountability too, not punishment, but responsibility as empowerment. If the food automation system is wasting 15% of production, that's not someone's failure to hide, it's a problem to solve together. Maybe the sensors need recalibration. Maybe the distribution algorithm needs adjustment. Maybe we need different crops. But we can only solve it if we can see it.

And cooperation. If NexaWorld in Argentina discovers a more efficient solar panel mounting system, that knowledge gets shared immediately with attempts in Chile, Spain, Australia. If someone in one city develops a better conflict resolution framework, it gets documented, tested elsewhere, refined collectively.

Open source isn't just for code, it's for entire systems of living. Because if this works, we don't want one successful city. We want thousands, each learning from the others.
<br />

### Human-Centered Design

Cities should exist to serve people, not the other way around. Not to serve capital. Not to serve efficiency metrics divorced from human experience. Not to serve abstract ideals.

This means automation and AI exist to eliminate unnecessary labor: the repetitive, the soul-crushing, the work that drains rather than energizes. Why should a human spend 40 hours a week doing something a robot can do better, faster, and without exhaustion? So they can "earn a living"? That's the old logic.

What if we flipped it? How do we unleash what people are capable of when they're not just surviving?

Some people will want to research. Others will teach. Some will make art, music, or explore physical limits through sport. Others will improve the systems themselves: better automation, better design, better coordination.
<br />

### Emotional Infrastructure

Technical systems and governance frameworks matter, but communities are fundamentally emotional ecosystems. People experience conflict, burnout, grief, jealousy, and value drift. Even with needs met and meaningful work available, humans are complex.

It requires emotional infrastructure: structured conflict resolution processes, psychological support systems, and spaces for both solitude and connection.

This is experimentation too. What conflict resolution methods actually work at 100+ people? How do you prevent charismatic individuals from hijacking collective decision-making?

Different communities will need different approaches based on their cultural contexts and the personalities involved.
<br />

### Sustainability & Circularity

Zero-waste isn't just an environmental talking point, it's a design approach that forces better thinking. When you can't throw anything "away" (because there is no "away"), you have to think in cycles, not lines.

Consider packaging. Right now, we design single-use containers, ship them globally, use them once, and they become pollution. A linear system. Instead, imagine standardized, reusable containers optimized for efficiency. Imagine logistics systems designed to return, clean, and redistribute them. Imagine local 3D printing producing replacements as needed from recycled material.

Or clothing. Fast fashion produces 92 million tons of textile waste annually. Why? Because clothes are designed to be disposed of. Imagine instead garments designed to be modular, repairable, upgradeable. Local micro-factories producing custom-fit clothing from bio-based materials that biodegrade safely when they finally wear out.

Every resource must be renewable or part of a closed loop.
<br />

## Questions & Challenges 🤔

### "How do you fund this initially?"

Through capitalism, and that's the entire point of the transition model. NexaWorld will be funded through donations, altruistic private investment, personal savings from participants, and anyone who wishes to contribute. This isn't a contradiction; it's pragmatism. We use current economic structures to acquire land and build initial infrastructure: housing, automation systems, energy generation, food production facilities.

This transition might take 15 years, 45 years, maybe more, maybe less. Once operational, these mini-cities become self-sustaining through their own productivity plus revenue from surplus sales, eco-tourism, and other sources. We want to create a pathway, not pretending we can ignore existing economic reality.
<br />

### "But who will do the unpleasant work?"

Technology handles what it can: food production, meal preparation, cleaning, energy management, routine monitoring. The goal is to leverage available robotics and automation to minimize necessary human labor for basic needs, not eliminate all work.

Yes, some human work remains: complex repairs, healthcare, teaching, creative problem-solving, system improvements. The scale and specific labor needs depend on available resources. Maybe 100 people, maybe 400-1000, depending on land area, funding, and project scope.

But here's the crucial difference: the entire system is designed to minimize necessary survival labor while respecting human diversity and potential. There will still be some work, but imagine being able to pursue what you actually want while preserving your mental wellbeing and following your ambitions (ideally ambitions that align with what the community needs).

Exercise routines designed by people who love teaching fitness. Engineers researching and solving problems not because money compels them, but because that's what drives them. Musicians teaching music because sharing that joy is inherently rewarding. Artists creating because that's what artists do.
<br />

### "How does money work inside NexaWorld?"

Ideally, it doesn't, at least not for basic needs. You don't pay for housing, food, healthcare, or education within the community. These are provided through the system's design. External revenue (surplus sales, eco-tourism) funds infrastructure and expansion, but isn't the mechanism for internal resource allocation.

How do communities coordinate access to non-essential resources? That's implementation-specific. The goal is minimizing monetary friction for daily life while maintaining viable economic interface with the external world.
<br />

### "What if people don't contribute?"

This concern assumes a problem that may not exist at the scale and selection process we're proposing, but it's worth addressing thoughtfully.

First, self-selection changes everything. People choose to join NexaWorld and can choose to leave. We're not trying to fix capitalism for everyone, we're building an option for people genuinely aligned with these values. That filtering alone transforms the dynamics.

Second, the specifics will vary by implementation. Each mini-city will design its own approach based on its context, size, and the people involved. There's no universal answer because the optimal solution depends on local conditions and community values.

A basic framework: Thoughtful selection based on cooperative alignment, transparency as accountability, voluntary exit options, and understanding root causes rather than punishment. Different implementations will discover what works.
<br />

### "Why will this succeed where communes failed?"

Technology fundamentally changes the equation. Historical communes often failed because insufficient productivity required too much human labor just for survival, economic isolation prevented financial sustainability, and ideological rigidity prevented adaptation when reality contradicted beliefs.

NexaWorld addresses these differently: modern automation can dramatically reduce survival labor requirements, revenue generation maintains economic viability, and evidence-based iteration allows continuous adaptation. We're not claiming guaranteed success, we're building testable models that learn from historical failures.

The transition strategy is key. We're not rejecting markets entirely or demanding ideological purity. We're using available tools (including capitalism) to build infrastructure that demonstrates different organizing principles.
<br />

### "What about the challenges you haven't addressed?"

Many. Governance at scale, economic resilience during downturns, the messiness of human nature that defies rational systems. This manifesto focuses on the first experiment - 100-200 people learning what works. Each challenge you can think of is valid, and we won't know how to address most of them until we try.

That's not naivety. The alternative is endless theorizing with no data. We're proposing to generate that data.
<br />

### "How do you handle truly scarce resources?"

This is where the rubber meets the road. Food, housing, energy - these can scale with technology. But what happens when someone needs unaffordable surgery? Or a drug that's difficult to produce? What about the researcher whose time is limited, or the specialist who can only help one person at a time?
<br />
We don't have perfect answers, but we have principles:
<br />

* Transparency in scarcity - When resources are genuinely limited, that limitation should be visible to everyone. Hidden scarcity breeds resentment; known scarcity enables collective problem-solving.
* Evidence-based allocation - Decisions guided by effectiveness data, not ability to pay. When choosing between options, the data informs the choice - success rates, impact metrics, resource efficiency - not wealth, status, or politics.
* Community input on values - Some allocation questions are ethical, not technical. Does the community prioritize prevention over intervention? Long-term research over immediate needs? Individual care over collective infrastructure? These are values questions that require collective decision-making, not top-down mandates.
* Reducing scarcity where possible - When we encounter scarcity, the question becomes: can we expand capacity? Train more people? Fund research into alternatives? Improve systems? The goal isn't managing permanent scarcity fairly, it's systematically working to eliminate bottlenecks.
<br />

Will this satisfy everyone? No. Will people disagree about priorities? Absolutely. But at least the disagreements happen in the open, guided by evidence and shared values, rather than hidden behind bureaucracies and pricing mechanisms that pretend to be neutral but aren't.
Different implementations will handle this differently based on their community values and available resources. That's okay, we're experimenting, not mandating.
<br />

## The Call 📣

Building even a single mini-city requires expertise across architecture, sustainable agriculture, energy systems, psychology, governance design, materials science, automation, economics, and more.

This manifesto is an invitation to collaborate. To contribute knowledge and skills. To experiment together. To question assumptions, including the assumptions in this document. To build proof that a better system is possible, not through revolution or waiting for collapse, but through demonstration.

NexaWorld isn't claiming to have solved how society should be organized. It's proposing that we test whether different organizing principles can work at small scale. Each implementation will teach us something. What works? What fails? What surprises us? Open source means sharing both successes and failures.

My personal goal is to acquire land and implement the first NexaWorld community, funded by my work in technology. But this vision goes beyond any individual.

NexaWorld is a call to engineers, scientists, artists, educators, and dreamers to build together a new paradigm for living: human-centered, self-sufficient, and guided by reason and compassion.
<br />

If you're interested in being part of this, reach out. We don't know if this will work. But we know the current system isn't optimized for human flourishing. That's reason enough to experiment.
